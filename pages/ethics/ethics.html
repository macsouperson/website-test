My OPINION on Islamic Ethics

Note: the following is not an academic discussion of Islamic Ethics, but rather a discussion of my opinions

Islam was founded in a society mired by inequality, the Islamic narrative chides Meccan society for its preoccupation with hording wealth, engaging in unfair business practices and not care for the most misfortunate among them. It was in this social mileau that Islam emerged, and thus we should interpret it as a religion founded in the principles of social justice. Unfortunately, that term has be caricaturized in our present day. But at it's core the Prophet's (SAW) message was intended, in part, to enjoin this followers to care for their fellow person.

Consider the zakat while often considered charitable giving, it is ostensibly a tax on those Muslims whose wealth exceeds a certain amount. It is a means of re-distributing this wealth to the poor and indigent. It is not dissimilar to, though not entirely the same as, the progressive tax systems implemented in many countries that offer robust social safety nets to their citizens. Relatedly, the Qur'an repeatedly enjoins Muslims to care for the poor and orphans

With this framework in mind, I am thoroughly convinced that Islam is a religion grounded in the principles of social justice. It is incumbent on Muslims to care for all peoples, and ensure they they are afforded basic dignities.

There is a subtle yet alluring trap when discussing the ethics and values that Islam promotes. Adherents, and to a greater degree, apologists will employ highly anachronictis arguments about Islam's impact on Meccan and Medinan societies. They will cite real, substantive change in the position of women, the poor, the enslaved, and other marginalized groups. And they are correct in pointing out the strides towards equity that Islam made among these groups. However, at the same time it must be noted that Islam by no means advocated for an equal or democratic society. It acknowledged and perpetuated discriminatory practices against the previously marginalized groups.
