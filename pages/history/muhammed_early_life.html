The advent of Islam as a formal religion began in 7th century Arabia with the Prophet Muhammed. The biographical works of the Prophet's life are referred to the sirah. We will in our retelling attempt to avoid the more hagiographical elements of the narrative of the Prophet's life. This does not by any means diminish his accomplishments or successes. Rather, one might argue that it grounds Islam more firmly as evidenced-based faith. Additionally, including these stories do not bolster Islam's claims and serve little purpose in building an understanding of Islam. For instance, in Ibn Ishaq's Sirah, he narrates that the story of the Prophet's marriage where 'Abd Allah ibn Abu Mutallib, prior to marrying Amina ibn Wahb, was proffered by a woman, Umm Qattal, who wished to bear his children. He declined, and after consummating this marriage once again met the same woman who showed no interest in him, claiming that he no longer held the same light. The seerah literature goes on to say that the Prophet's mother "saw a light at the time of his birth which illuminated the palaces of Syria" (Musnad Ahmad 17151). Early Life of the Prophet

Birth

While it is unknown exactly the year of his birth, some have attempted to tie it to auspicious events in Mecca, namely the Year of the Elephant (570 CE), when invaders, led by the King Abraha, came to destroy the ka'ba riding elephants, but were deterred by a flock of stone-bearing birds:

Have you not seen O Prophet how your Lord dealt with the Army of the Elephant? Did He not frustrate their scheme? For He sent against them flocks of birds, that pelted them with stones of baked clay, leaving them like chewed up straw. (Quran, al-Fil)

The Prophet was born in the city of Mecca, as a member of the Quraysh. The Quraysh held a priveldeged position among the Arabs as stewards of the pilgramige and the ka'ba. His father was 'Abd Allah ibn 'Abd al-Muttalib, and his mother was Amina bint Wahb of the Banu Zuhra. The Prophet's grandfather 'Abd al-Muttalib, was well-respected among the Quraysh and had 10 sons, with 'Abd Allah being the youngest. 'Abd al-Muttalib's was afforded special respect among the Quraysh, due to the fact that he restored the wells of zam zam, which had previously unearthed by the prophet Ishmael.

The Prophet's father died around the time of his birth, and he remained under the care of his mother, Amina and had two wetnurses who cared for him as well, Thuwayba and Halima. Wetnursing was part of a larger tradition for Meccan Arabs, in which newborns were sent to reside in the desert. This was to ensure that city-dwelling Meccan did not lose their connection to the land and did not become dominated by the doldrums of a sedentary life. According to Ibn Ishaq, when Halima, along with other women of the Bani Sa'd ibn Bakr arrived at Mecca, none would accept the Prophet as he was considered an orphan, due to the death of Abd Allah ibn al-Muttalib. The exchange between the bediouns tribe and the Quraysh also had the utilitarian purpose of connecting families. A child that was nursed by a women was ostensibly a member of that family. While this forbade the union of those who shared the same wetnurse, it brought them together as family. In fact, later in life the Prophet would reject a marriage proposal on these grounds, as the woman in question had also been nursed by Thuwayba. Halima, having found no other child to nurse, took in the Prophet to nurse and he stayed with them for two years. This length of time to stay with a Bedioun tribe was not considered exceptional and some children stayed even longer.

Childhood

His mother passed around the sixth year of the Prophet's life, while returning from a trip to Yathrib. After her death he was cared for by his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib. Prophet Muhammed remained with Abd al-Muttalib for for the next two years, until his grandfather passed away. With the death of his grandfather, the Prophet was entrusted to his paternal uncle Abu Talib, who shared both the same mother and father as Abd Allah. The control of the zam zam well and the disbursement of the water to pilgrims was passed from Abd al-Muttalib to his youngest brother al-Abbas.

Like Abd al-Muttalib, Abu Talib loved and cared for the Prophet like a son of his own. Abu Talib lacked the standing of his father, and was not particularly wealhy. Abu Talib like many of the Meccan Arabs supported himself, in part, as a merchant, and the Prophet would accompany him on his commercial expeditions north to Syria, and the greater Levant.

Early Adulthood

When the Prophet had reached adolsence, conflict had broken out between two tribes, the Kinana and the Qays Aylan. It was dubbed the fijar or sacriligious war as the inciting incident involved a member of the Kinana tribe killing Urwa al-Rahhal, of the Hawazin and by extension the Qays Aylan. Urwa was attacked and killed by al-Barrad of the Kinana due to a conflict involving trade. The murder occuring during the sacred month, when killing was expressply forbiddien, thus earning the ensuing war the moniker of 'sacriligous war'. Fighting broke out between the Hawazin and the Kinana, with the Quraysh fighting on the side of the Kinana. As was typical, fighting typically lasted only a few days, though the fijar persisted over a number of years, continuing through the Prophet's twentieth year. With the Quraysh's involvement in the fijar, Abu Talib and the Prophet were involved as combatants. There were four battles during this period, the battle of Shamta, the battle of al-Abla, and at al-Sharb. The last battle was at al-Harira, afterwhich the tribes agreed to end the conflict, after a blood payment.

Marriage & Family

The Prophet had gained a reputation for truthfulness and fine charecter, earning the moniker al-Amin. His reputation had reached the, Khadija bint Khuwaylid, who hired him to lead her trading caravan to Syria. Following this trip, Khadija offered her hand in marriage to the Prophet and they were wed. The Prophet was married at the age of 25, with narrations varying significantly on Khadija's age. According to some, she was also 25, or possibly 35 while Tabari stated she was 40 at the time of the marriage. Together, Khadija and the Prophet Muhammed had 7 children, three sons, al-Qasim, al-Tahir, al-Tayyib1 and four daughters, Ruqayya, Zaynab, Umm Kulthum, Fatima. None of the Prophet's sons survived into adulthood, with the narrations diverging on when each passed away. The Prophet also had another son, Ibrahim, with Maria the Copt, a slave from Egypt.

As the Prophet and Khadija, she gifted him a slave a young man named Zayd bin Harithah and the Prophet freed his own slave, Barakah. While Zayd was given to the Prophet as a slave, he was ostensibly adopted and know in Mecca as Zayd ibn Muhammed. The Prophet and Khadija's home was frequently visited by his cousin Safiyyah and her son Zubayr, as well Salma who had helped Khadija with the delivery of all her children. Halima, the Prophet's wetnurse also visited.

While the Prophet's marriage to Khadija had afforded him some upward economic mobility, this uncle Abu Talib struggled to support his family financially. The issue became particularly acute during a famine which struck the Hejaz. The Prophet, alongside his uncle 'Abbas each took in one of the Abu Talib's children. 'Abbas took Abu Talib's second youngest son Ja'far, and the Prophet did the same for the youngest, 'Ali.

Evidence of the Prophet's growing reputation as al-Amin was that Abu Abu Lahab, two of the Prophet's daughters, Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum married the sons of Abu Lahab, Utbah and Utaybah, a half-brother to Abd Allah.

Khadija had a cousin, a Christian monk named Waraqa ibn Nawfal. He was a learned man and familiar with the Scriptures.
