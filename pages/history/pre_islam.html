Pre-Islamic Arabia

So, rather than languishing on the pre-Islamic period, it will suffice to say that the Arabian peninsula and surrounding Levant were home to diverse groups. The Arabian peninsula and surrounding area were religiously and culturally diverse. The the west were Christian Byzantines, and to the east were the Zoroastrian Sassanians. Within the region, were Christian, Jews, pagans, and monotheistic hanifs. Urban centers such as Mecca were centers of commerce and were surrounded by nomadic Bedouin tribes, sharing in a ___ relationship. Mecca served as both a commercial and spiritual hub. The ka'ba, a holy site for the surrounding tribes, housing dieties from several of the pagan religions afforded Mecca and its tribes certain distinctions.

Ka'ba

The ka'ba according to the Islamic tradition, was first built by the prophet Ibrahim and his son Isma'il. Ibrahim was also the one to instantiate the ritual of the hajj. Despite it's origins, the ka'ba soon became associated with polytheism and the worship of idols. It acted as a warehouse of religous iconography. Worshippers would make the journey to Mecca to complete either the hajj or umra, which included practices such as circumabulation of the ka'ba and running between the mounts of al-Safa and al-Marwa. These practices were continued in the Islamic tradition. Throughout history the ka'ba has been rebuilt, including during and after the lifetime of the Prophet (Ka'ba EI3). Quraysh

Long before the time of the Prophet's birth the Quraysh had firmly asserted their control in Mecca, through both their authority of the pilgramige to the ka'ba as well as a control over regional trade. Entry into the ka'ba and the provision of food and drink to the pilgrims were duties of the Quraysh.
